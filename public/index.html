Ripeness

Watermelon is a popular and refreshing fruit that belongs to the Cucurbitaceae family. It is a vining annual plant that is native to Africa and has been cultivated for over 4,000 years. However, it's not always easy to determine if they're ripe.

01.

Ripe Watermelon

A ripe watermelon is typically characterized by a few distinct signs. One of the best indicators is a large, creamy yellow spot on the underside of the melon, which is where it rested on the ground during ripening. This spot is often referred to as the "ground spot."

02.

Unripe Watermelon

An unripe watermelon is one that has not yet reached its full maturity and ripeness. It is typically have a pale or white flesh, and they may not have the characteristic sweet and refreshing flavor of a ripe watermelon. Researchgate notes that unripe watermelons have lower levels of beta carotene, lycopene, and other nutrients compared to ripe ones.

Learn more
03.

Overripe Watermelon

An overripe watermelon is one that has passed its peak ripeness and is beginning to deteriorate. Overripe watermelons may have soft spots on the surface, and the flesh may be mushy or watery. They may also have an unpleasantly sweet or sour taste, and the texture may be unappealingly soft or grainy.

01.

Sugar-baby Watermelon

Sugar Baby watermelon is a popular heirloom variety of watermelon that is known for its compact size, sweet flavor, and ease of growth. Sugar Baby watermelon is a reliable and easy-to-grow variety that is perfect for small gardens, containers, and beginners. It is also a good choice for those who want to harvest watermelons in a relatively short period of time.

02.

Crimson Watermelon

Crimson Sweet watermelon is a variety of watermelon that is round-to-oval in shape, averaging 25 lbs. in weight, with a rind having light green and dark green stripes. The inside has sweet, firm, bright, deep red flesh that is high in sugar content and contains very few, small seeds. It is one of the best-tasting of large melons with many disease-resistant traits. It was introduced by Kansas State University in 1963. Watermelons originated in Africa thousands of years ago.
